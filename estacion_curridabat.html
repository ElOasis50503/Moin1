Cogiendo Café, Finca de F. Castro

Picking coffee, Castro's Farm, Curridabat
Cogiendo café. Finca de F. Castro — Curridabat — Picking Coffee. Castro's Farm

Five pickers work a row of mature coffee trees, each carrying a woven harvest basket strapped at the waist — the standard tool of the trade, sized to hold a substantial weight of cherries while leaving both hands free to work the branches. Three women in long skirts and aprons stand at left, a man in a broad-brimmed hat reaches into the canopy at center, and a fifth figure, partly obscured by foliage, works the row at right. The trees themselves are densely planted and heavily laden, rising well above head height — characteristic of an established, mature planting rather than a young or recently pruned one.

The postcard's trilingual-style caption block — Spanish above, the town name centered, English below — is typical of the export-oriented postcard production aimed at foreign visitors and the diaspora market, the same convention seen across the Tres Ríos coffee postcards from a few miles back up the line. Naming the proprietor directly ("Finca de F. Castro") rather than leaving the farm anonymous suggests either a locally well-known holding or a card produced with the farm owner's own commercial interest in mind.

Curridabat: Coffee at the City's Edge

Curridabat marks the last real stretch of agricultural countryside the Atlantic Railway passes through before reaching San José — close enough to the capital today to function as one of its suburbs, but in the postcard-production era documented here, still distinctly its own farming town, with the coffee economy that defined most of the Central Valley's rural identity. This card's specificity is its most interesting feature: rather than a generic "coffee harvest" scene, the caption names the farm directly — Finca de F. Castro — giving this plate a level of documentary precision that most period postcards, content to sell an impression of tropical agriculture to a foreign market, didn't bother with.

The five pickers and their woven baskets represent the same labor system documented more expansively elsewhere along this stretch of the line — the Tres Ríos postcards a few miles back show the same baskets, the same family- and community-based harvest labor, the same export economy running from tree to basket to ox-cart to beneficio to sack to railcar to the docks at Limón. Curridabat's own larger harvest-crew portrait, logged from earlier research but not yet held for this build, shows that same system at a much larger scale — a single farm's entire workforce, men, women, and children together, posed beside a loaded cart. Between this card's five named pickers and that crew's twenty-five or thirty anonymous ones lies the full range of how the coffee harvest was actually organized: sometimes a small, named operation, sometimes the entire available labor of a community.

The hub's own line table places Curridabat at 161.0 km / 100 miles from Limón — just 4.6 km short of the San José terminus. By the time a train reached this point, the long Caribbean lowland run, the Reventazón gorge, and the climb over Ochomogo were all behind it; only the final approach into the capital remained.
