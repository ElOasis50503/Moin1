Mile 70 — a depot on a forested hillside, a banana stalk tall enough to dwarf the man photographed beside it, and ox-carts queued at a working trapiche, three glimpses of a single Reventazón-valley town's working economy.

About this spoke: Tucurrique sits on the descent between Turrialba and the lower Reventazón corridor, a valley town whose economy ran on a mix of rail freight, banana cultivation, and traditional sugar processing. This page gathers three plates spanning roughly two decades of that economy: the station itself, with its corrugated-iron depot building and a push-car waiting on the siding; a striking 1920s photograph of a banana plant at the Hacienda de Tucurrique, tall enough to dwarf the man posed beside it; and a 1923 image of a working trapiche, ox-carts delivering freshly cut cane to be milled beneath a tall masonry chimney.

The Depot

TUC-001 Date unconfirmed · F.C. al Atlántico postcard

Estación de Tucurrique

Depot building, freight cars, and push-car
F. C. Al. Tucurrique, C.R. (no. 376)

The depot building sits on a shelf cut into a forested hillside, its corrugated-iron roof and walls lettered "TUCURRIQUE" in bold capitals on the gable end facing the platform. A long covered platform extends from the depot toward a freight shed further along the track, where two boxcars stand coupled together on the siding. In the foreground, a four-wheeled push car (carro de empuje) sits on the near track, the kind of hand-propelled inspection vehicle used for light maintenance runs between stations — the same type of equipment documented at several other stops along this stretch of the line. Additional wooden buildings, including what appears to be a water tower or storage structure, stand at the platform's far end, with terraced hillside dwellings visible in the wooded slopes beyond.

The postcard format — printed with a sequential number (376) and the abbreviated line designation "F.C. Al." (Ferrocarril al Atlántico) — places this among the same commercial real-photo-postcard production that documented stations across the line for sale to travelers and the diaspora market, the same genre represented by several other depot views gathered elsewhere in this supplement.

The Hacienda

TUC-002 1920–1930

Plantío de Banano, Hacienda de Tucurrique

A record banana stalk, with figure for scale

A man in a wide-brimmed hat and work clothes stands at the base of a banana plant whose broad, ragged leaves rise several times his height, fanning out against the sky in a dense, almost architectural canopy. A single fruit bunch is visible hanging at the upper right of the frame, partly obscured by the surrounding foliage. The composition — a human figure deliberately placed at the base of an exceptionally tall specimen — is a recognizable convention of agricultural photography from this period, used to demonstrate the productivity or sheer scale of a planting for an audience that might never see a banana plant in person.

The original record names a "Silver Epriny" as the subject photographed at the Hacienda de Tucurrique, very likely a transcription or phonetic rendering of a name that hasn't been independently confirmed; the figure's identity remains an open research question for this plate. What's clear from the image itself is the agricultural ambition the photograph was meant to convey: this was banana cultivation presented as a showpiece, not merely a working crop.

TUC-003 1923

Trapiche en Tucurrique

Ox-carts delivering sugarcane to a traditional mill

An ox stands yoked to a heavy-wheeled cart piled high with freshly cut sugarcane, a second loaded cart visible just beyond it, both queued before a long open-sided shed roofed in corrugated zinc. Workers move among additional carts and milling apparatus under the shed's cover, while a tall, whitewashed masonry chimney rises at the center of the complex — evidence of a boiling house substantial enough to require real industrial ventilation rather than a simple open fire. Further milling equipment, including what appears to be a smaller cart or hopper fitted with its own iron-wheeled undercarriage, stands near the chimney's base.

The 1923 date places this trapiche squarely in the same era as the Stanley Lindo sugar estate documented at Juan Viñas elsewhere in this supplement, part of a broader pattern of small-to-mid-scale sugar processing operations scattered through the Reventazón valley's coffee-and-cane economy. Where the Juan Viñas ingenio was photographed as an industrial complex with smokestacks and a company-town scale, this Tucurrique trapiche reads as a more modest, traditional operation — animal-powered cart transport feeding a single boiling house, rather than a railway-served export mill.

Tucurrique: A Valley Town's Mixed Economy

Tucurrique sits on the long foothill descent between Turrialba and the lower Reventazón corridor, a stretch of the line documented elsewhere in this supplement mostly through bare infrastructure — cuttings, mile markers, electrical poles. The three plates gathered here give Tucurrique itself a fuller picture than most of its neighboring stops: not just a depot, but a working agricultural economy with at least two distinct crops in active production at the same moment in the town's history.

The banana plate and the trapiche plate, photographed roughly contemporaneously in the early 1920s, capture that economy at two different scales and in two different registers. The Hacienda de Tucurrique banana plant, posed deliberately with a human figure for scale, is showpiece agriculture — the kind of photograph meant to demonstrate productivity to an audience that might never see the crop growing. The trapiche, by contrast, is documentary rather than promotional: ox-carts queued at a working mill, cane delivered and processed in the ordinary course of business, with no figure posed for effect. Together they suggest a town diversified enough to support both an export-oriented plantation crop and a traditional, locally consumed one, a mix not every station along this line could claim.

The depot itself, lettered plainly "TUCURRIQUE" on its corrugated-iron gable, anchors both economies to the railway that served them — bananas and sugar alike needed the line to reach any market beyond the immediate valley. The hub's own line table places Tucurrique at 112.7 km / 70 miles from Limón, roughly the midpoint of the line's long Reventazón-corridor stretch between Turrialba and the upper Cartago basin.
