About this spoke: El Alto de Ochomogo is not a named station with its own mileage entry in the line table — it is the height of land itself, the pass between the Cartago basin and the rest of the Valle Central that the railway, and the highway after it, must cross to link the two halves of the line. It is documented here as its own spoke because of its singular role in the corridor's geography and history, distinct from any single station's working life.

El Alto de Ochomogo — the Continental Divide, near Cartago
A Bilingual Sign Marks the Height of Land near Cartago

A rail-adapted motorcar pauses beside the bilingual sign marking the Continental Divide, at 1,539 meters (5,049 feet) — the highest point on the line between Limón and San José. The sign records the remaining distance in both directions: 93.2 miles to Limón, 10.0 miles to San José. This is El Alto, where the watershed splits: streams rising east of the pass flow to the Caribbean, those rising west flow to the Pacific. For much of its hundred-mile length the railway runs alongside the Reventazón River.

Vehicle is a rail-adapted motorcar — a road-car body fitted with flanged wheels and a railroad-style cowcatcher grille — rather than a locomotive proper. Published as a plate in The National Geographic Magazine; exact issue and year not yet confirmed.

El Alto: The Line's Decisive Threshold

Every other spoke in this supplement documents a place the railway passes through — a depot, a mill town, a river crossing. El Alto de Ochomogo is different: it is the line's decisive geographic threshold, the point at which the entire character of the journey changes. East of the pass, the descent toward Cartago, Turrialba, and the Reventazón corridor begins the long fall to the Caribbean lowlands; west of it, the line drops instead toward the Valle Central and San José. The watershed itself runs through the pass — a fact the bilingual sign photographed here states almost as a public notice, marking not just distance but the literal divide between two oceans' worth of rainfall.

The pass carries weight beyond the railway. Alto de Ochomogo has been a contested high ground in nineteenth-century Costa Rican civil conflict, and remains today the conventional threshold between Cartago and the rest of the Valle Central — a status this catalogue's own Cartago spoke notes directly, observing that the descent from San José "descends through El Alto (Ochomogo) and crosses the Puente del Fierro (Iron Bridge) just before reaching the station." This plate, with its formally surveyed and signed crossing, documents that same height of land at a different moment than the catalogue's earlier glass-plate negative of January 1925 — which shows the pass open and unmarked, its road receding unbounded across the high country. Together the two images bracket a period in which Ochomogo went from an unmarked rural pass to a properly surveyed, signed, and administered point on the national rail network.
