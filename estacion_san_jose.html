Mile 103 — the western terminus of the Ferrocarril al Atlántico, where Keith's line from the Caribbean ended (or began) at a mansard-roofed pavilion built to impress arriving travelers as much as to move coffee out of the Central Valley.

About this spoke: San José's Estación al Atlántico anchors the opposite end of the line from Limón and Moín — this was the terminus a passenger or a sack of coffee would reach only after the full 165.6-kilometer descent (or climb) between the Caribbean coast and the Central Valley. The station building itself, completed in the 1900s under Minor Keith's railway enterprise, was as much a civic statement as a piece of transport infrastructure, and its postcard record reflects that ambition.

San José, C. R. — Estación al Atlántico

San José terminus, three-quarter view with waiting carriages
San José, C. R. - Estación al Atlántico (Edición Tormo)

The station presents its full mansard-roofed length on a three-quarter angle, the elaborately fretworked eaves and dormered roofline catching the light above a deep colonnaded veranda. Three horse-drawn carriages wait in the foreground at the foot of the entrance steps, their drivers and a loose cluster of passengers, porters, and onlookers gathered in the gap between them — the ordinary traffic of a terminus expecting an arrival.

The building's architectural ambition is the point of the image as much as the railway itself: turned finials, a balustraded gallery, and a clock-faced gable mark this as civic architecture built to impress, not merely a shed for freight. As the western terminus of Keith's Ferrocarril al Atlántico, this was the building through which San José's coffee economy connected to the Caribbean port of Limón and, from there, to the wider world — the counterpart, at 165.6 km distant, to the line's other end documented on the Moín and Limón spokes.

San José: The Terminus as Statement

If Moín and Limón mark where the Atlantic Railway began — geographically and historically, since construction proceeded inland from the coast — San José's Estación al Atlántico marks where the project's ambitions were ultimately validated. A line built at brutal human cost through Henry Meiggs Keith's abandoned contract, Minor Cooper Keith's 1883 commitment, and nineteen years of construction across some of the country's most difficult terrain finally delivered its passengers and cargo to a building designed to look the part: mansard roofs, fretworked eaves, a balustraded gallery, and a clock-faced gable, all assembled in a style meant to announce that Costa Rica's capital had arrived in the age of rail.

The postcard record of the station, of which this plate is one example, consistently frames the building from a three-quarter angle with carriages waiting in the foreground — a composition that does double duty as both architectural portrait and a quiet advertisement for the modernity the line represented. The horse-drawn carriages in this particular view are themselves a small dating clue, situating the photograph in the station's earlier decades before motor transport became commonplace at the terminus, much as the rubber-tired oxcarts at Juan Viñas mark a comparable transitional moment further down the line.

The hub's own line table places this terminus at 165.6 km / 103 miles from Limón — the full length of the descent (or, from this end, the climb) that the Juan Viñas, Turrialba, and other intermediate spokes document piece by piece. As the western bookend to Limón and Moín at the line's Caribbean end, this station closes the loop on the spoke series: every intermediate stop on the line ultimately led somewhere, and this was where it led.
