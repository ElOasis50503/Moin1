Estación de Las Lomas

Las Lomas, Limón Province — locomotive approaching through brush, head-on view

A steam locomotive comes on directly toward the camera, all but swallowed by the surrounding coastal bush and by its own exhaust — a heavy, near-vertical column of steam rising from the stack and folding back over the cab. The engine's headlamp and pilot are barely legible through the haze of smoke and the soft focus of the print; the dense vegetation crowding both sides of the right-of-way underscores how thoroughly the line at this point cut through lowland forest rather than open ground.

The framing and exposure suggest the photographer was working quickly, likely from trackside as the train approached — a working shot rather than a posed one, and a useful counterpoint within this supplement to the more composed station and bridge views recorded elsewhere on the line.

Las Lomas, Limón Province — locomotive No. 52 at the depot and water tower

The same locomotive, or one very like it, now at rest in the yard, its number — 52 — legible on the smokebox front. The engine stands beside the water tower's timber trestle, its tank and spout visible at right, while a short string of freight cars and what appears to be a caboose trail off toward the depot building under the hillside in the background. Two figures are visible near the cars, suggesting routine yard work rather than a special working.

Where the head-on plate captures the line in motion through dense bush, this view supplies the working infrastructure that made such movement possible — water, siding, and depot — and gives Las Lomas its own concrete identity within the line's lowland stations rather than leaving it as a name and a mile figure on the table alone.

Las Lomas: A Working Stop in the Lowland Bush

Las Lomas sits at mile 46 from Limón, a modest station on the long, low-grade stretch where the Atlantic Railway still ran close to sea level, well before the line began its real climb into the Cartago highlands. Unlike Juan Viñas or Turrialba, no single industry or estate defines this stop in the documentary record; what survives instead is a more elemental picture of the railway simply doing its work — a locomotive moving through thick lowland vegetation, and that same locomotive standing in the yard beside the water tower that kept it running.

The two plates gathered here read naturally as a before-and-after of a single working day, or at least a single kind of working day: the engine pushing through brush on its approach, steam pluming hard against the humidity of the coastal lowlands, and then the same engine at rest, number 52 plainly stenciled on its smokebox, taking on water and idling beside its short train. Neither image carries a publisher's imprint or a dateable inscription, and both are catalogued here as personal-collection scans pending further identification — but together they document something the more polished station portraits elsewhere in this supplement do not: the unglamorous, continuous labor of keeping a steam railway moving through terrain that gave it no easy passage.

Las Lomas's position in the line — between La Junta at mile 39 and Bonilla at mile 52 — places it squarely within the stretch of track that ran through the Bonilla y la Montaña en Movimiento landscape documented elsewhere in the Atlantic Railway archive, lending these two plates a useful geographic anchor even without further provenance.
