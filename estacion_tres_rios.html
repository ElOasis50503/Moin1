Mile 97 — the last substantial coffee town before San José, where Minor Keith's own name turns up on sacks bound for export, and the harvest still moved by ox-cart between farm, beneficio, and rail.

About this spoke: Tres Ríos sits squarely in the coffee country between Cartago and San José, close enough to the capital to function as a satellite town and far enough into the Central Valley's best growing elevation to anchor a serious export economy of its own. This page gathers three postcards from that economy: sacked coffee ready for shipment at Finca Keith — Minor Cooper Keith's own holding — an ox-cart unloading the harvest by hand at a beneficio loading dock, and the Dulce Nombre road on the town's outskirts, the kind of ordinary rural infrastructure that moved both the coffee and the people who grew it.

Café para Exportar, Finca Keith

Coffee ready for shipment, Sanchez
Café para exportar. Finca Keith — Sanchez — Coffee ready for shipment

A beneficio warehouse interior, its open timber-frame roof strung with what appears to be drying or storage netting overhead, packed floor to head-height with stenciled burlap coffee sacks — some marked "C.K." and other partial lettering suggesting grade or lot codes. A single worker in a flat cap stands among the stacked sacks near the center of the frame, giving the pile its scale, while more sacks and what looks like processing or weighing equipment occupy the right side of the image.

The name on the card is not incidental: "Finca Keith" almost certainly refers to a holding of Minor Cooper Keith himself, the railway concessionaire whose name appears throughout this project's Atlantic Railway material — the same Keith whose 1883 contract finally committed Costa Rica to the Limón–San José route this whole supplement documents. A coffee finca bearing his name at Sanchez, near Tres Ríos, is a small but pointed reminder that the men who built the railway were very often also the men who owned the land and crops the railway existed to move.

The Roads of Tres Ríos

TR-002 Date unconfirmed · postcard

Unloading Coffee

Ox-cart unloading at a beneficio loading dock, Tres Ríos
Unloading coffee. Tres Rios, C.R.

A worker stands inside the deep wooden bed of an ox-cart, shoveling coffee cherries down through a chute toward a second worker crouched below, who guides the falling fruit into a pair of wooden sorting crates set at ground level. The cart itself is a substantial piece of equipment — high-sided, iron-banded, mounted on a single massive wooden wheel with a turned hub, built to carry a full harvest load over rough roads. A yoke of two oxen stands patiently to the right, still hitched, with the hills around Tres Ríos rising hazily in the background.

The scale of the cart and the volume of cherries visible inside it suggest this is a beneficio receiving dock rather than a farm gate — the point in the supply chain where the harvest first changes hands from grower transport to processing facility, immediately before pulping, fermentation, washing, and drying would begin. The same basic cart design — large single rear wheel, high iron-strapped sides — recurs across this project's other Caribbean and Central Valley agricultural material as the standard heavy-haul vehicle of the era.

TR-003 Date unconfirmed · bilingual postcard

Camino Dulce Nombre

Dulce Nombre Road, on the outskirts of Tres Ríos
Camino Dulce Nombre — Tres Ríos — Dulce Nombre Road

A dirt road curves gently between low fences and a stand of bare-branched trees — likely poró or a similar deciduous species used regionally as living fencing, leafless in this view but clearly mature and well established. Two riders on horseback or mules travel the road at a walk, with a third animal, possibly riderless or a pack mule, visible at the far left edge of the frame. The composition is unhurried and entirely rural, the kind of ordinary country road postcard publishers used to round out a regional series alongside the more obviously commercial agricultural and infrastructure subjects.

Dulce Nombre is itself a small locality on the edge of Tres Ríos' built-up center, and this card's quiet, undramatic road scene serves as a useful counterweight to the working images elsewhere on this page — a reminder that the coffee economy depicted in the Finca Keith and unloading plates existed inside an ordinary rural landscape of roads, fences, and unremarkable daily travel, not only inside warehouses and loading docks.

Tres Ríos: Coffee at the Capital's Doorstep

Tres Ríos sits at the closest practical distance to San José that still counted as proper coffee country in the postcard era — close enough that produce could reach the capital's markets and the export rail line within hours, far enough into the surrounding hills to grow coffee at the elevation the crop demanded. The three cards gathered here trace that economy from three different points of view: the warehouse where the finished, sacked product waited for shipment; the loading dock where the raw harvest first arrived by ox-cart; and the ordinary country road that carried both people and produce between the farms and the town center.

The Finca Keith plate carries the most pointed historical detail of the three. A finca bearing Minor Cooper Keith's name at Sanchez, just outside Tres Ríos, is a reminder that the railway's central historical figure — the man behind the 1883 contract that finally committed Costa Rica to the San José–Limón route this whole supplement documents — was never simply a builder of infrastructure. He was also, by the time these postcards were produced, a substantial landowner in exactly the kind of coffee country his own railway existed to serve, a convergence of transportation interest and agricultural ownership that runs through the entire history of the Atlantic line.

The unloading and Dulce Nombre plates round out the picture at a more human scale: a single ox-cart's worth of harvest changing hands at a beneficio dock, and two riders making an unremarkable trip down a tree-lined country road. The hub's own line table places Tres Ríos at 155.7 km / 97 miles from Limón — just 9.9 km short of the San José terminus, close enough that a traveler reaching this point would have known the journey, and the descent from the Caribbean lowlands through Reventazón country and over Ochomogo, was very nearly finished.
