The Cacao Trade

MAT-001 1975

Drying Cacao, Matina

Cacao beans on a rolling-roof drying rack, Limón Province

A worker rakes a bed of cacao beans spread across a long wooden drying tray, turning the harvest under open sky to cure evenly in the sun. The tray sits beneath a corrugated-iron roof built on a pivot or rolling track — visible in the angled, weathered structure to the left — designed to be pushed back over the beans at the first sign of rain, a simple piece of vernacular engineering that remained the standard small-grower drying technology across the Caribbean cacao zone for most of the twentieth century. A second, larger building with a similar rust-streaked roof stands behind, with coconut palm and banana foliage crowding the edges of the clearing.

The date — 1975 — places this image roughly midway between the early-twentieth-century postcard record of the Matina cacao trade (H.N. Rudd's published views of terraced drying tables and pod-breaking crews) and the disease pressures of the 1980s and 1990s, when monilia and frosty pod rot devastated Costa Rica's traditional cacao plantings. Photographed only a decade or so before that decline began in earnest, this plate documents the trade close to the end of its long, comparatively unbroken run on the Caribbean lowlands.

Matina: A Lowland Town Built on Cacao

Matina sits in the riverine lowlands between Limón and Siquirres, a stretch of the Atlantic Railway corridor that, unlike the sugar and coffee country further inland, organized itself around cacao. The crop predates the railway by more than two centuries — cacao cultivation along this stretch of coast traces back to the colonial period, when it was one of the few exports the Caribbean side of the country could sustain on its own terms, long before Minor Keith's line connected it efficiently to the world. The railway's arrival in the 1880s didn't create the cacao economy at Matina; it simply gave an existing trade a faster road to Limón's docks.

The photographic record bridges two very different eras of the same basic process. H.N. Rudd's early-twentieth-century postcards depict the trade at an almost-industrial scale — terraced drying tables cascading down a slope, crews breaking pods by the riverside, the kind of systematic documentation that suggests corporate or publicity interest as much as casual tourism. The 1975 photograph gathered here under "The Cacao Trade" shows something more modest and more durable: a single rolling-roof drying rack, a single worker, the same fundamental technology persisting essentially unchanged across six or seven decades. Between those two images lies the whole texture of smallholder cacao farming on the Caribbean coast — visible at scale in postcards meant to sell an industry, and visible at the level of one rack and one harvest in a family snapshot.

What the 1975 date also marks, in retrospect, is a kind of closing chapter. Within a decade or two of this photograph, monilia and frosty pod rot would devastate Costa Rica's traditional cacao plantings across the Caribbean lowlands, and much of the acreage that had supported towns like Matina for generations would convert to banana or simply go fallow. The hub's own line table places Matina at 35.8 km / 22 miles from Limón — a short hop on the railway, but, in the cacao economy's own terms, close to the heart of the whole enterprise.
