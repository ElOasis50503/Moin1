Mile 37 — a working junction town on the Caribbean lowland run, photographed once at its ordinary daily business and once dressed in flags for an occasion the record itself leaves unexplained.

About this spoke: Siquirres has historically been the best-documented single locality on this stretch of the line, sitting on the main Caribbean lowland run a few kilometers short of La Junta, where the Línea Vieja toward Guápiles actually branches off toward Toro Amarillo. This spoke gathers two plates: a dated 1946 station-yard view under the "Siquirres Restaurant" sign, and a separate, undated occasion photograph showing the platform decorated with flags and bunting before a substantial crowd. Four further Siquirres plates logged from earlier research — an early streetscape (1910), a banana-export locomotive lineup (1906), the "I. Boris" wholesale building (1930), and a 1970 Independence Day parade at the Escuela Justo Antonio Facio — are referenced here and reserved for future addition.

The Yard, 1946

SIQ-001 1946

El Patio de la Estación

Station yard and platform, with the Siquirres Restaurant sign

A covered platform recedes under a corrugated-iron canopy strung with overhead cabling, a sign reading "SIQUIRRES RESTAURANT" mounted prominently at the near end — confirming that the station functioned as a proper meal stop on the line, not merely a passing halt. On the siding at left, a numbered caboose or crew car (legible as "1014") stands ahead of a string of boxcars stretching back along the track. Several railway workers walk the platform toward the camera, one figure accompanied by what may be a dog at the lower right edge of the frame, with station buildings and a palm-thatched roofline visible in the middle distance.

The 1946 date is firm rather than estimated, giving this plate real value as a fixed point for what the Siquirres yard's equipment and layout looked like at mid-century — a useful anchor against the undated, more festive plate gathered below, and against the four further Siquirres items logged from earlier research but not yet held for this build.

A Decorated Occasion

SIQ-002 Date unconfirmed · reproduced 1991

Un Día de Fiesta en Siquirres

Decorated platform and crowd, occasion unidentified

A substantial two-story building along the track — its upper gallery crowded with onlookers and what may be greenery or palm fronds draped along the railing — is hung floor to platform with national flags and bunting strung in repeating swags between the support posts. A sign reading "SIQUIRRES..." is mounted above the crowd at center, with a second partial sign legible as "C. AUBREY SHAW" to its right, suggesting a commercial building — likely the drugstore or general store identified in earlier cataloguing of this same Siquirres material. Dozens of people, men in hats and shirtsleeves predominating, fill the platform and spill onto the foreground track, several seated on what may be makeshift benches or crates, in a crowd large enough to suggest an organized civic or commercial occasion rather than an ordinary travel day.

The specific event isn't identified by the photograph itself, and the date carries only the "reproduced 1991" note from its source rather than a firm original date — but the scale of the decoration and the crowd place this comfortably among the kind of official visits, inaugurations, or civic celebrations that period photographers in railway towns reliably turned out to document. Paired with the dated 1946 yard view above, this plate adds Siquirres' more ceremonial face to its working one.

Siquirres: The Lowlands' Working Town

Siquirres sits on the long Caribbean lowland run, far enough inland from Limón to have escaped the port city's full cosmopolitan churn but close enough to the action of the line to become, by most accounts, the single best-documented town between the coast and the Reventazón gorge. A few kilometers further on, at La Junta, the original Línea Vieja toward Guápiles and Toro Amarillo branches away from the main trunk — making Siquirres less a junction itself than the last substantial stop before one, a working town defined by through-traffic rather than by being a destination in its own right.

The two plates gathered here capture two faces of that working town a generation or so apart in feel, if not necessarily in calendar time. The 1946 yard view is Siquirres at its most ordinary — boxcars on the siding, a restaurant sign over the platform, workers walking past on whatever business the day required. The decorated occasion plate, by contrast, shows the same kind of platform transformed for a moment into civic theater: flags, bunting, a crowd large enough to fill the frame, gathered for an event the photograph itself doesn't name. Local memory of Siquirres' station years, recorded in oral histories from residents who grew up there in the 1970s and 80s, recalls exactly this duality — the railway as unremarkable daily transport for bananas and people, punctuated by the fried fish, yuca, and local sweets sold at the station, alongside its occasional role as the site of larger community gatherings.

The hub's own line table places Siquirres at 60.0 km / 37 miles from Limón — solidly inside the Caribbean lowland stretch, with the Reventazón corridor's climb toward Cartago and San José still well ahead. Four further plates documented from earlier research — an early streetscape from 1910, a 1906 banana-export locomotive lineup, the 1930 "I. Boris" wholesale building, and a 1970 Independence Day school parade — remain logged for this spoke and reserved for future addition, which would round Siquirres out as the richest single-locality record on this entire supplement.
