Estación de LimónLimón Province · 0.0 km / Mile 0 — the Atlantic terminus

The coastal terminus where the line to San José began — station, port, and town, gathered from seven photographs spanning roughly five decades

↩ The Full Line The Station The Port The Town History

Mile zero — the journey's coastal end, where passengers boarded for the climb to San José and the United Fruit Company's ships waited at the company pier.

About this spoke: Limón is documented here through seven photographs of varied provenance and date, spanning roughly the 1880s through the 1930s — institutional railway records, a United Fruit Company promotional postcard, a press stereoview, a studio street scene, and a privately held colorized reproduction. Rather than a single coherent series, this page gathers the fullest picture available of the terminus town: the station itself, the company docks that gave the port its commercial purpose, and the streets and market that made it a town rather than merely a railhead.

The Station

LIM-001 1930s

La Estación, Años 30's the station building, since demolished

The Limón station building itself, seen from the trackside: two-story timber construction with a deep wraparound veranda on the upper floor, a steep multi-gable roof, and a wide canopy extending over the platform where multiple rail lines fan out toward the foreground. Two figures walk the tracks; a palm grove stands behind the building.

The original print carries a colorized, canvas-textured reproduction treatment rather than a raw period photograph — a later art-toned copy made for sale or display, distinct from the 1880–1900 creation estimate that applies to the building and scene depicted. The Archivo's own title confirms the building was demolished in the 1980s, making this and its companion platform view (LIM-002) documentation of a structure that no longer exists.

LIM-002 Undated (early–mid 20th century)

La Plataforma a passenger boarding mid-leap, station canopy at right

A candid, in-motion view of the platform in use: a man in a wide-brimmed hat is caught mid-leap, swinging up into the open door of a passenger carriage, one boot still in the air, hand gripping the doorframe. A woman in a long dress and broad straw hat stands watching at left; down the platform, men in white shirts and hats gather under the deep eaves of the station roof, with a row of onlookers or porters at the platform's edge on the right. The roof bracket and signal pole at far right anchor the shot as the station building proper — the same structure documented from outside in LIM-001.

This is institutional railway documentation rather than a postcard, credited to the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles. The Archivo's date range (1900–2018) is a database placeholder rather than a genuine estimate; the clothing and carriage style are consistent with general Atlantic-line passenger service in the early-to-mid 20th century.

The Port

LIM-003 1922

Muelle United Fruit Company the railway meets the Caribbean trade it was built to serve

A United Fruit Company steamship docked at the company pier, rigged with cargo booms and a passenger awning aft. Railway tracks run directly onto the pier in the foreground, with a small rail car visible mid-frame. Three women in white Edwardian-era dresses pose on the dock — two standing near the rail line, one seated on the heavy dock-fender rope coils at the water's edge — while warehouse and shed buildings line the dock at left.

The trilingual caption block — "Muelle United Fruit Company / Puerto Limón / United Fruit Co. Docks" — identifies this as United Fruit's own promotional photography rather than institutional railway or Archivo documentation, a different provenance from most of the other plates on this page. The track running directly onto the pier visually ties the rail corridor to the Caribbean shipping it existed to serve: bananas out, manufactured goods in.

LIM-004 1904

Vendedoras del Tren fruit and bread sellers boarding food to passengers, "COSTA RICA" lettered on the car

Two women in white dresses balance laden trays on their heads — fruit, bread, wrapped food parcels — walking trackside to sell to passengers leaning out the windows of a passenger car lettered "COSTA RICA" along its upper panel, with "SEGUNDA" (second class) visible on the lower car body. A man at left reaches up toward one vendor mid-transaction; passengers crowd the windows watching, and porters and onlookers fill out the platform, one in a pith-style helmet at right.

The rounded top edge and dark corner vignetting are characteristic of a commercial stereoview card, cropped from what was very likely a stereo pair shot for 3D viewing — a different format and market from the institutional and United Fruit material elsewhere on this page. The Archivo's own creator field credits a Facebook archival-rescue source rather than a traditional deposit, the same acquisition path as LIM-001.

The Town

LIM-005 circa 1880–1900 (approximate)

Gran Hotel, Limón a three-story timber hotel at "mile zero"

A three-story timber hotel of considerable scale for the period, wrapped on every level in deep verandas trimmed with jigsaw-cut valances along each eave. Three small dormered cupolas break the roofline; a lower wing extends toward a stand of royal palms at right, where the building steps down to meet the grade. The close-cropped lawn and single entrance rail suggest formal hotel grounds rather than a working yard.

The Archivo's own reference label tags this image "Limón, Mile zero," and a hotel of this scale so early in the town's development speaks to Limón's intended role as a gateway requiring lodging for engineers, officials, and the first wave of commercial rail and shipping traffic.

LIM-006 1922

Calle Principal the main street, under a single dominant shade tree

A wide dirt street running under the canopy of a massive shade tree that dominates the whole frame, with a horse-drawn cart crossing mid-street. A man stands at left near a shopfront whose sign reads "Films, Developing, Views" — a photography and film-supply shop, an incidental detail given the role such studios played in producing images like the ones gathered on this page. On the right, a substantial two-story masonry building with iron balconies carries a partially visible sign reading "PARK," almost certainly the Park Hotel, contrasting with the timber-and-veranda construction of the Gran Hotel (LIM-005) on the opposite side of the street.

The source collection — Colección Manuel Gómez Miralles — connects this image to other Gómez Miralles material logged elsewhere in the wider Costa Rica Histórica catalogue system, including a postcard plate in the Coyol, Ganado y Caña agricultural catalogue.

LIM-007 Undated

Mercado de Limón the market plaza, clock tower behind the trees

A wide plaza shaded by several mature trees, with the long, low, open-sided market structure visible behind — its corrugated roof running well back into the frame — and a distinctive square clock tower rising just behind the largest tree, a real architectural landmark for the town. A cyclist crosses the foreground, a vendor pushes a cart near the curb, and pedestrians move along the sidewalk near an advertising placard at the tower's base.

This is the only plate on this page held in Craig's own collection rather than the Archivo Nacional — a Foto Shop postcard, identified by the handwritten studio caption "Mercado de Limón / Foto Shop. Costa Rica," a publisher distinct from Gómez Miralles, the United Fruit Company, and the institutional ICF/Archivo material elsewhere on this page. The civic market building and clock tower fill out the town's commercial infrastructure alongside the hotels (LIM-001, LIM-005) and street scene (LIM-006).

Limón: The Terminus Town

Of all the stations on this line, Limón is the one that was never just a station. Every other stop on the Atlantic Railway exists because the line passes through it; Limón exists because the line ends there, at the Caribbean port the entire railway was built to reach. The seven photographs gathered on this page reflect that difference in kind: where Las Lomas and La Junta are documented through narrow, technical glimpses — a locomotive at a depot, a washed-out embankment — Limón is documented as a town in its own right, with a hotel, a market, a main street, and a working harbor alongside the railway infrastructure proper.

The provenance of these seven images is itself a kind of map of how a port town got photographed. The institutional railway record (LIM-001, LIM-002) comes from the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles and Archivo Nacional collections built for technical or administrative purposes. The United Fruit Company dock view (LIM-003) is commercial promotional photography, produced to advertise the company's own infrastructure. The 1904 stereoview of food vendors (LIM-004) belongs to an entirely different market — stereo cards sold for home entertainment, not institutional or corporate documentation. And the town views — the Gran Hotel, Calle Principal, and the Mercado (LIM-005 through LIM-007) — come from three further sources again: an unattributed early contact print, the Gómez Miralles studio, and the Foto Shop postcard house, the last of these the only plate on this page held outside the Archivo Nacional altogether.

Two threads run across nearly every plate. First, timber: the Gran Hotel's jigsaw-trimmed verandas, the 1930s station building's wraparound gallery, and the contrast with the masonry Park Hotel on Calle Principal all trace a town built mostly in wood, with masonry construction arriving later and unevenly. Second, the railway's centrality to everything else: even the food vendors of LIM-004 and the dockworkers implied by LIM-003 exist because the line and the port created a population to feed and a cargo to move. The station building demolished in the 1980s (LIM-001) is the clearest reminder that Limón's terminus-town character was not a permanent feature of the built environment — only of the place's function on the line.
