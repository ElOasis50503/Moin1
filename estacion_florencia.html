Mile 66 — a curving cut through a wooded hillside near Florencia, electrical line strung overhead on simple wooden poles, a single unidentified figure walking the right-of-way alone.

About this spoke: Florencia sits on the descent between Turrialba and the Reventazón corridor's lower stretch toward Cartago, in foothill country where the line cuts repeatedly through wooded ridges rather than running flat. The plate gathered here documents one such cut at Mile 66, near Florencia itself, with a telegraph or power line following the track and a house visible at the bend in the distance — ordinary infrastructure photography of the kind the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles produced routinely to record the line's physical condition mile by mile.

Línea Ferroviaria, Milla 66

Near Florencia, Cartago Province

The track curves gently leftward through a shallow cut, a steep, densely wooded hillside rising on the right side of the frame while a more open, scrub-covered slope falls away to the left. A line of wooden poles strung with electrical or telegraph wire follows the right-of-way on the left side of the track, with a tall royal palm and the gable end of a house visible at the bend where the line disappears around the hillside. A single unidentified figure stands on the track partway down the cut, dwarfed by the surrounding terrain — the kind of incidental human presence that gives an otherwise purely infrastructural photograph its sense of scale.

The original archival description identifies this specifically as Mile 66 in the vicinity of Florencia, with the electrical line and the distant house noted as the photograph's secondary subjects alongside the track itself. Photographs of this type — close, unglamorous documentation of a specific mile marker rather than a named station or notable engineering feature — recur throughout the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles' archival record of the line, evidently produced as routine condition surveys of the right-of-way rather than as commemorative or promotional images.

Mile 66: The Line as Subject

Florencia, like several of the smaller way-points documented in this supplement, left almost no trace in the kind of postcard and promotional photography that survives in abundance for the major stations and the towns built around sugar mills, coffee fincas, and banana plantations. What survives instead, here, is something more modest and in some ways more honest: a working photograph of the right-of-way itself, identified not by a station name but by its mile marker — Mile 66 — with Florencia noted only as the nearest named place.

This kind of photograph belongs to a distinct genre within the Atlantic Railway's archival record, one this project has encountered at several other unnamed or barely-named points along the line: condition surveys, infrastructure inventories, or simple documentation of a stretch of track, electrical line, or cutting, produced by the Instituto Costarricense de Ferrocarriles for institutional rather than commemorative purposes. The single figure walking the track in this image, like the unidentified figures in several comparable plates elsewhere in this project's Caribbean and foothill material, is almost certainly incidental to the photograph's actual purpose — a worker going about ordinary business who happened to be in frame when the shutter opened, not a subject the photographer set out to capture.

The hub's own line table places Florencia at 106.4 km / 66 miles from Limón, on the long foothill descent between Turrialba and the lower Reventazón corridor — a stretch of line defined less by any single dramatic feature than by exactly this kind of repeated, workmanlike cutting through wooded ridge after wooded ridge, each one looking very much like the last, and each one, evidently, worth a photograph to the institution responsible for keeping the line running.
