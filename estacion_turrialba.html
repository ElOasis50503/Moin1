A long line of ox-carts, each piled high with sacked coffee under canvas covers, curves across an open field toward the camera, the queue stretching back far enough to fade into the tree line at the horizon — a striking demonstration of scale that few of this project's other coffee-economy postcards attempt. At lower right, a worker stands beside a long masonry channel or tank, possibly part of a washing or fermentation system associated with the beneficio, with a low retaining wall and what may be a sluice gate visible along its near edge. Wooded hills rise behind the whole scene, framing the open field where the carts have assembled.

The caption names the same proprietor — F. Castro — as the Curridabat coffee-picking card catalogued elsewhere in this supplement, raising the strong possibility that this was either a single operation with holdings in both localities, or a name recognized widely enough across the coffee trade to appear on multiple postcards from different towns. Whether one farm or a connected family enterprise, the pairing gives this supplement an unusual thread: the same name recurring at two points along the line, each time at harvest time, each time framed for an export-postcard audience.

Turrialba: The Threshold Town

Turrialba occupies a different kind of position on the Atlantic Railway than most of the stations documented in this supplement. Where Juan Viñas reads almost entirely as company town, built around the Stanley Lindo sugar estate, and Matina and Siquirres organized themselves around cacao and the lowland banana trade respectively, Turrialba anchors a genuinely diversified valley economy — coffee, cattle, and cane all coexisted here, and the town's position partway down the line's long descent made it a natural staging point where produce from the Central Valley's agrarian world met the railway that would carry it toward the Caribbean export economy below.

The F. Castro coffee plate captures exactly that staging function: not a farm or a finca shown in isolation, but a long line of ox-carts queued before what appears to be processing infrastructure, the harvest gathered and ready to move. The same proprietor's name on a companion card from Curridabat — at the opposite end of this supplement's geography, near the San José terminus rather than the Caribbean lowlands — suggests either a single coffee operation with holdings spanning a remarkable stretch of the line, or simply a name well enough established in the regional coffee trade to anchor postcards from more than one town. Either reading reinforces the same point: the coffee economy this railway served wasn't confined to any single locality, and a grower's reach could span much of the line's length.

The hub's own line table places Turrialba at 102.4 km / 64 miles from Limón — roughly the midpoint of the full descent, with the Reventazón corridor's well-documented landslide and flood history (catalogued in detail in this project's other Cartago-province spokes) running through the stretch immediately around it.
