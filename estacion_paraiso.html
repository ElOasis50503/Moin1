Estación de Paraíso

Paraíso, Cartago Province — depot building, gable and platform elevation

A wooden station building presents its gable end toward the camera, with a long open-sided platform canopy extending away under a single corrugated-metal roofline. A carved bargeboard trims the gable peak, and a two-line sign board is mounted on the gable face beneath it — the lettering is not legible at this resolution and is left unread rather than guessed. A smaller framed notice, also illegible, is posted on the wall near the platform-side windows.

A wrought-iron picket fence and gate enclose the building's near end, with a tall pole — likely telegraph or signal wire — rising just beyond the platform edge. A bicycle and rider are faintly visible at the image's far right margin. The print itself bears a small star-shaped decorative mount or seal affixed near the foreground track, along with visible emulsion wear in that area — a feature of the physical photograph rather than the photographed scene, and not a depicted rail mechanism.

Paraíso: A Second View

Paraíso already holds a place in this catalogue's Corredor del Reventazón division through the pipeline-construction panorama catalogued earlier in the project — a view of civic infrastructure work in progress, with the town as backdrop. This plate complements that one with a different subject entirely: the station building itself, the fixed point around which the corridor's rail traffic, agricultural exports, and town life converged.

Paraíso sits within the same demanding Reventazón valley geography that runs through Peralta, Torito, and Tucurrique elsewhere in this division — terrain the catalogue has already framed, in the cataloguer's own words, around the recognition that "the Atlantic side will never be defeated," with Route 32's modern landslide vulnerability standing as the contemporary echo of the line's historic engineering challenges. A station depot like this one, however modest, was the corridor's basic unit of infrastructure: the place where that engineering effort met daily use.

Further identification — a legible reading of the gable sign, a date, and a source attribution — would let this plate carry the same level of documentary confidence as the catalogue's better-anchored station plates (Juan Viñas, Peralta). Until then, it is presented with its uncertainties stated plainly rather than resolved by assumption.
