Salón Comunal de Cimarrones

Barrio de Cimarrones, Pacuarito, Distrito de Siquirres

A simple wooden building with a steep corrugated-iron gable roof stands at the edge of a clearing, dense secondary forest crowding close behind it and a second, larger structure visible at right beyond the trees. Boarded shutters cover the windows on the building's long side, and a hand-painted notice is lettered directly onto one of the front shutters — legible, if irregularly spelled, as a warning that entry is strictly forbidden to all without exception. A modest covered entry porch with a low gate occupies the building's front corner, and construction debris and scrap lumber litter the bare ground in front of the structure.

Cimarrones appears in the historical FECOSA station listing as one of the named localities along the Caribbean lowland stretch of the line between Pacuarito and Madre de Dios — geographically and administratively tied to Pacuarito rather than warranting its own line entry, much as the photograph's own caption identifies it: a barrio of Pacuarito, within the broader district of Siquirres. Unlike the depot buildings and right-of-way views that dominate this supplement's other Caribbean lowland plates, this image documents civic rather than railway infrastructure — the kind of small community hall that would have served local meetings, gatherings, or administration for a settlement otherwise defined almost entirely by the banana plantations and the rail line passing through it.

Pacuarito and Cimarrones: Beyond the Railway Frame

Most of the stations documented in this supplement survive in the photographic record because the railway made them worth photographing — a depot building, a curve of track, a junction's switching geometry. Pacuarito and its barrio of Cimarrones are documented here differently: not through railway infrastructure at all, but through a single image of community life at the most local possible scale, a wooden hall with its windows boarded and a hand-lettered notice barring entry. The historical FECOSA station listing places Cimarrones among the named stops on the Caribbean lowland stretch between Siquirres and Limón, alongside Madre de Dios and Veintiocho Millas — small settlements that existed because the railway and the banana plantations it served created population centers, even where no grand depot building or notable engineering feature ever justified its own postcard.

The Salón Comunal itself is unremarkable as architecture — a simple gable-roofed structure differing little from countless similar buildings across rural Costa Rica — but its condition in this photograph, closed and boarded with an explicit notice against entry, hints at a small institutional story the image alone can't fully tell: a hall built for community use, now shuttered, for reasons the photograph doesn't specify. Whether that closure reflects disuse, dispute, or simply a temporary state at the moment of photographing, the building stands as a reminder that the Caribbean lowland corridor this railway served was inhabited by people whose civic and communal lives extended well beyond what the trains themselves carried.

The hub's own line table places Pacuarito at 54.7 km / 34 miles from Limón, between Batán and Siquirres — a station this spoke is built to eventually document on its own rail-infrastructure terms as well, once a depot or right-of-way plate for Pacuarito proper surfaces to join this Cimarrones civic record.
