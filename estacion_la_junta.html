Mile 39 on the climb from Limón — the fork where the original line to Guápiles split from the route to the capital, and where the Reventazón nearly won.

About this spoke: La Junta is documented here through a single dated survey pair from December 1928, recording the washout and partial destruction of the track on the east side of the Reventazón bridge. The two photographs — numbered N.4 and N.5 in the original survey sequence — were taken in close succession: one looking across the river through the bridge truss at the severed embankment, the other from track level back toward the La Junta station building itself, with workers gathered at the torn edge of the cut.

The Bridge

LJ-001 December 1928 · survey photograph "N.4"

El Puente del Reventazón view from the river, looking through the truss toward the severed embankment

The Reventazón river bridge at La Junta, seen head-on from the water through its iron truss. On the embankment at right, the rails hang twisted and unsupported where the river has undercut the fill beneath them, curving down into open air at the very edge of the cut. A compass arrow inked at the top of the original print ("Oeste → N") records the shot's orientation for the survey file.

The handwritten caption beneath the original photograph, in period Spanish, records the event in the surveyor's own words: the washout and destruction of the track on the east side of the Reventazón bridge at La Junta, where the current scoured away thirty meters of the fill supporting the line, leaving the bridge isolated at both ends.

"Dicbre. 1928 — Lavado y destrucción de la vía al lado E. del puente del Reventazón en La Junta. La corriente lavó 30 m. del relleno que soporta la vía. Por sus 2 extremos el puente quedó aislado."

The 1928 Washout

LJ-002 December 1928 · survey photograph "N.5"

La Estación, Vista desde el Corte view from track level, looking back toward the La Junta station building

The companion view to LJ-001, taken from the same washout looking the opposite direction: back along the severed track toward the La Junta station, its gable end lettered "LA JUNTA" plainly visible above the building's raised first floor. A small crowd — railway workers or onlookers — stands at the very edge of the eroded cut, with loose rails stacked at angles down the torn embankment where the fill has given way beneath them.

The original caption continues the account begun on LJ-001, extending it: the same December 1928 current that destroyed the track on the bridge's east side also undercut two houses — one on the south side of the line, one on the north — leaving the bridge isolated at both of its ends.

"Dicbre. 1928. Lavado y destrucción de la vía al lado O. del puente del Reventazón en La Junta. La corriente arrastró 2 casas que había al lado Sur, y otra al lado Norte. Por sus dos extremos el puente quedó aislado."

La Junta: The Fork in the Line

The name itself announces the station's role: La Junta, "the junction." This was the point where the Atlantic Railway's main route toward San José — climbing west along the Reventazón through Florida, Las Lomas, and Peralta toward Turrialba — split from the older "Línea Vieja," the original lowland branch continuing north toward Guápiles and Toro Amarillo. A washout here was never a purely local misfortune: damage at this single junction could isolate traffic on two branches at once, with passengers and freight bound for either Guápiles or the capital equally stranded.

The December 1928 event documented in this pair was severe by any measure. The Reventazón's current did not merely undermine an embankment; it carried away thirty meters of fill beneath the track on the bridge's east side and, per the second photograph's caption, swept away two houses standing near the line — one south of the right-of-way, one north. With both ends of the bridge left isolated, repair crews would have faced the same basic problem documented elsewhere on this line's Turrialba/Reventazón division: rebuilding fill against a river that, as this catalogue's own throughline has it, "will never be defeated."

The two photographs are survey documentation rather than commercial or press photography — directional compass arrows, sequential plate numbers (N.4, N.5; print numbers R 634 and R 632), and the terse, factual tone of the handwritten captions all point to an engineering or maintenance department record rather than a postcard or newspaper source. Read together, they preserve both the technical fact of the damage and, in the gathered figures of LJ-002, the human response to it: workers standing at the very edge of the break, assessing what would need to be rebuilt before the line could run again.
