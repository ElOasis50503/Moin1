Plantación de Cabuya, Santiago

Santiago, Cartago Province — sisal/cabuya stand, M. Peralta estate
Plantación de cabuya. M. Peralta. Santiago / Sisal Plantation. M. Peralta.

A dense stand of mature cabuya (sisal, Agave sisalana or a related fiber agave) fills the foreground, its long spiked leaves radiating outward in the characteristic agave rosette. A man on horseback moves through the plantation with at least one other rider or worker visible further back, giving the image a working-estate quality rather than a purely botanical one.

The trilingual captioning convention — Spanish caption, place name, English caption — matches the Orosí tobacco plate (002164) catalogued elsewhere in this corridor, suggesting both were produced for the same kind of promotional or export-trade publication aimed at showcasing Costa Rican agricultural exports to foreign audiences.

Hauling the Harvest

SANT-002 CR-AN-AH-FO-002163-1 · 1922

Acarreando Cabuya, Santiago

Santiago, Cartago Province — ox-cart hauling, processing shed
Acarreando cabuya. M. Peralta. Santiago / Hauling sisal. M. Peralta.

An ox-drawn cart, its solid stone wheel typical of the period's heavy farm transport, stands loaded with bundled cabuya fiber or harvested leaves beside an open-sided shed. The angled poles visible under the roofline are consistent with fiber-drying racks, a standard stage in sisal processing after the leaves are crushed and the raw fiber extracted.

Two figures stand near the cart and a third approaches along the yard, giving a sense of the estate's working scale. Paired with SANT-001, this plate completes the documentary pair: cultivation in the field, then harvest and processing at the estate buildings — the same plantation-to-product structure seen in this corridor's other agro-export plates (Orosí tobacco, Tucurrique cane, Batán abacá).

Santiago and the Corridor's Fiber Economy

Santiago's place in the Reventazón corridor's agricultural record is narrower than Tucurrique's cane or Orosí's tobacco, but it fills out the same picture: a landscape organized, plot by plot, around a single export crop and the estate that processed it. Cabuya — sisal or a closely related fiber agave — supplied cordage and rough textile fiber for both domestic use and export, a less prominent but still significant strand in Costa Rica's early twentieth-century commodity economy alongside coffee, sugar, tobacco, and banana fiber.

The M. Peralta plantation name is worth keeping distinct from the Peralta rail locality documented elsewhere in this catalogue's Corredor del Reventazón division — a personal surname and a place name that happen to coincide, not the same entity. The two photographs gathered here, cultivation and harvest, follow the same documentary logic as the catalogue's other agro-export pairs: showing the crop standing in the field, then the labor and infrastructure that turned it into a marketable product.

Both plates carry the trilingual promotional captioning style seen elsewhere in this corridor's agricultural record, suggesting they were produced as part of a coordinated effort — likely an investor prospectus or agricultural exposition publication — to present Costa Rican export agriculture to audiences abroad, with the railway corridor itself as the implicit connective infrastructure making that export trade possible.
