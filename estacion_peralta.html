Mile 55 — a two-story station house on the Reventazón corridor, photographed in its working prime, on a stretch of line the river beside it would spend decades trying to reclaim.

About this spoke: Peralta sits on the Cartago/Limón provincial boundary, close enough to the Reventazón to be repeatedly drawn into the river's flood history — most notably in 1949, when the Reventazón's overflow washed out the embankment between Peralta and neighboring Torito, leaving the rails twisted and suspended over open ground. This page documents both halves of that story: the station building in its intact working years, and the flood-damage record from the 1949 disaster gathered below.

The Depot

PER-001 Date unconfirmed

Estación de Peralta

Station façade with platform, staff, and pump trolley
PERALTA

The two-story depot presents its full façade to the platform, "PERALTA" lettered across the ground-floor wall in raised block capitals, with twin iron-railed balconies projecting from the upper story on either side and a deep, fretwork-trimmed gable roof above. A separate single-story platform shed stands to the left along the same track, and the running rails curve away through dense forest on both sides of the clearing — the line still cut hard against the bush this far down the Reventazón corridor.

In the foreground, a man in shirtsleeves and cap stands beside the fence line while two more figures in hats and jackets pause near a flowering shrub, with at least one additional figure visible further back near the depot steps — a small gathering that reads as station staff and visitors rather than a posed formal portrait. A four-wheeled railway pump trolley, the kind of hand-propelled inspection car used for track maintenance and light running between stations, sits parked on the near siding in the right foreground, its lever mechanism visible above the frame.

The 1949 Flood

PER-002 1949–1950

Inundación de 1949, Peralta–Torito

Reventazón flood washout, embankment destroyed
Fotografía tomada después de la inundación de 1949 entre Peralta y Torito por el rebalse del río Reventazón.

The rails run straight toward the camera and simply stop where the embankment beneath them has been carried away entirely, the ties left twisted and hanging at odd angles over bare, eroded ground. A worker in a hat and pack stands at the edge of the washout, looking out over the swollen, debris-choked river beyond — the Reventazón itself, still visibly high and turbulent in this photograph, with felled trees and stripped vegetation along its banks testifying to the force of the flood that did this damage. A second figure and what may be a dog are visible at the frame's left edge, near a stand of trees that marks the limit of the flood's direct reach.

This is the companion plate to the station façade photographed above: where that image shows Peralta's depot intact and ordinary, this one shows the same general stretch of line reduced to twisted iron and washed-out fill, the embankment between Peralta and the next station at Torito simply gone where the Reventazón's floodwaters overran it. The dated range (1949–1950) and the explicit original caption naming both the location — between Peralta and Torito — and the cause — the Reventazón's overflow — make this one of the more precisely documented disaster plates in this entire supplement, comparable in kind to the Milla 84¼ landslide pair and the December 1927 flood material referenced elsewhere in this project's Reventazón corridor research.

Peralta: A Station on the Reventazón's Terms

Peralta sits on the boundary between Cartago and Limón provinces, on the long, geologically restless stretch of the Atlantic Railway that follows the Reventazón River down from the Central Valley to the Caribbean lowlands. The station building photographed here — a two-story depot with twin balconies and a fretwork-trimmed roof, more architecturally ambitious than the simple corrugated-iron sheds typical of the smaller stops further toward Limón — suggests Peralta carried more traffic, or at least more institutional investment, than its modest position on the line table might imply.

That investment was tested directly by the river the line ran beside. In 1949, the Reventazón's floodwaters overflowed and washed out the rail embankment between Peralta and the next station at Torito, leaving the track twisted and suspended over open ground where the fill beneath it had simply been carried away — documented in the second plate gathered above, which belongs to the same broader pattern Craig has noted elsewhere in this project: that the Atlantic side of the line was never permanently defeated by any single washout, but was never fully safe from the next one either.

The hub's own line table places Peralta at 88.2 km / 55 miles from Limón, on the Cartago/Limón provincial line — geographically and administratively a transitional point on the descent, and, as the flood record shows, transitional in a more literal sense whenever the Reventazón rose high enough to remind the railway who actually controlled that stretch of ground.
