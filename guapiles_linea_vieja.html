The Línea Vieja — the "old line" between Limón and Carrillo, the railway's first route into the banana country, left behind once the main trunk was rerouted up the Reventazón toward San José but never abandoned by the town that grew up along it.

About this spoke: Guápiles sits on the Línea Vieja, the original Limón–Carrillo branch built in the 1880s under Minor Keith's railway concession — a route distinct from the San José–Limón trunk this hub documents station by station, which is why Guápiles appears here as a side trip rather than a row on the main line table. This page is the first installment of a planned four-plate sequence following the town across roughly a century: a royal-palm avenue at the old Finca Salvador estate, this mid-century street scene with a steam locomotive working through the town center, a later photograph from the line's diesel era, and the station building's adaptive reuse as Guápiles' Casa de la Cultura. Only the mid-century steam plate is held for this build; the other three are logged and reserved for future additions.

Guápiles Centro

Main street and rail line, steam locomotive approaching
GUAPILES centro a mediados del Siglo XX. Foto: Claudio Cruz

The track runs straight up the middle of Guápiles' main street, with a row of wooden storefronts crowding the rail line on the left — a sign reading "...A PRINCESA Zapatería" (shoe shop) visible among them — and a steam locomotive approaching from the far end of the street, smoke just beginning to show above the boiler. Pedestrians walk alongside and across the track without much apparent concern for the oncoming train, two women in patterned dresses among them, while a dog trots across the rails in the foreground and children loiter near the shopfronts. A signal mast or water tower structure stands on the right side of the street, with royal palms rising above the rooflines against an overcast sky.

The casualness of the scene is the point: by the mid-twentieth century, the railway running directly through Guápiles' commercial street was simply part of daily town life, not a special occasion. Guápiles: The Old Line's Town

Guápiles began as Finca San Salvador, a single estate at the terminus of the railway's earliest push into the Caribbean lowlands. In 1879, during Tomás Guardia's second administration, the government signed an agreement with Minor Cooper Keith to extend the line as far as the Río Sucio, to a point that would be named Carrillo by law in 1883 — a route built specifically to move bananas, and the project that gave rise to the United Fruit Company's presence in Costa Rica. That Limón–Guápiles–Carrillo route became known, once the railway's main trunk was later built up the Reventazón corridor toward Cartago and San José, as the Línea Vieja — the "old line," left geographically aside but never abandoned by the towns strung along it.

The station building itself was constructed between 1900 and 1905 to a standard United Fruit Company design — gable roof, broad eaves on wooden posts, built entirely in timber and metal — the same template used across the company's other Caribbean lowland depots. Guápiles grew around it through cycles of boom and bust that tracked the banana economy directly: prosperity while the plantations thrived, decline when blight and disease hit the fruit, then a slower recovery as cattle ranching, sugar cane, and timber brought new colonists down from Cartago, Coronado, and Moravia to resettle land the bananas had left behind.

This plate — Claudio Cruz's mid-century photograph of the town's main street, rails running straight down the middle past shopfronts and pedestrians — captures Guápiles in what was probably its most settled, least dramatic phase: no longer the raw terminus of the 1880s, not yet the abandoned depot of the line's 1995 closure under the Figueres administration. The building's later chapters are well documented elsewhere and reserved for this spoke's still-pending plates: a diesel-era operational view, and the station's eventual second life as Guápiles' Casa de la Cultura, formally authorized in 2001 after decades of intermittent use and neglect following the railway's closure.
