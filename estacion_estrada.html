Mile 21 — the point where two routes out of Limón, the Ramal de Saborío and the Ramal de Zent, rejoined into a single line, making Estrada a junction station rather than simply a stop along the way.

About this spoke: Estrada occupied a structurally distinctive position on the Atlantic Railway's Limón-side network: out of Limón, the line split at Empalme Moín into two parallel routes — the coastal Ramal de Saborío and the inland Ramal de Zent, each serving its own string of small banana-country stops — and the two routes rejoined here at Estrada before continuing as a single line toward Matina, Bataan, and Siquirres. The two-story depot presents its gable end to the platform, a balcony with simple iron railing projecting from the upper story above a ground floor lettered "ESTRADA" in bold raised characters. A separate single-story building, likely a freight shed or secondary platform structure, stands just beyond it along the same curve of track. Several figures are gathered at the platform's edge near the station entrance, while the rails curve away in both directions at the foreground — track geometry consistent with a junction rather than a simple straight-line stop.

That curving double-track arrangement is the structural signature of what made Estrada distinctive on this stretch of the line: out of Limón, the railway split at Empalme Moín into two separate routes — the Ramal de Saborío running along the coast through Swamp Mouth and Miramar, and the Ramal de Zent running inland through Buffalo, Liverpool, and Zent itself — and the two branches rejoined here at Estrada before continuing as a single line toward Matina, Bataan, and Siquirres. Estrada: Where Two Lines Became One

Most stations on the Atlantic Railway are simple points on a single line — a stop the train passed through on its way somewhere else. Estrada was different. Out of Limón, the railway's earliest banana-country network split at Empalme Moín into two distinct routes serving the same general territory by different paths: the Ramal de Saborío ran along the coast, passing through Swamp Mouth, Miramar, and a string of small lowland stops, while the Ramal de Zent cut inland through Buffalo, Liverpool, and the settlement that gave the branch its name. Both routes existed to serve the dense banana plantation country threaded between Limón and the interior, and both converged again at Estrada, where they rejoined into a single line continuing on toward Matina, Bataan, and Siquirres.

That junction function shows up directly in the photograph gathered here: the curving double track visible at the depot's platform edge is the physical signature of two routes meeting at one point, not simply a passing siding. An Archivo Nacional land-transfer record for the Estación de Estrada property — naming Adán Saborío Quesada and María Alfaro Vargas as the parties who conveyed the land to the Northern Railway Company — corroborates this directly, and gives the Saborío branch its eponymous source: not an abstract place name, but an actual landowner whose holdings the railway crossed.

The hub's own line table places Estrada at 33.5 km / 21 miles from Limón, solidly within the Caribbean lowland banana zone this stretch of the line was built to serve.
