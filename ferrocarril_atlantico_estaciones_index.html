The Full Line Construction History

Three stops on a single descent — Cartago, Juan Viñas, and Turrialba — anchor a line that once carried coffee from the Central Valley to the Caribbean port of Limón, and the postcards that survive from each town record a railway still at the height of its working life.

Why this supplement exists: the Cartago, Juan Viñas, and Turrialba postcards in the collection depict towns that were also numbered stations on the Ferrocarril al Atlántico. This file is the line-wide hub: the full station table below, with a link to each station's own spoke page in the rightmost column, plus a general construction history. Station-specific detail, hazard history, and photographs live on the spoke pages themselves.

The Line — San José to Limón

At its fullest extent the Ferrocarril al Atlántico carried more than sixty named stations, sidings, and spurs between the two terminals, descending roughly 1,070 metres from the Central Valley to sea level over 165.6 km (103 miles). Historical Note

Building the Line, 1871–1890

The contract history behind these three stations is tangled even by the standards of nineteenth-century railway finance. Henry Meiggs signed the original 1871 construction contract, then immediately transferred it to his nephew Henry Meiggs Keith — in breach of the contract's own anti-assignment clause — before abandoning Costa Rica abruptly in 1873 amid unresolved questions about how construction funds had been spent. He left the Cartago–Alajuela section complete and in use, which is the section that first brought rail service to Cartago.

It fell to his brother, Minor Cooper Keith, to finish the job. Keith assumed the Limón–Siquirres and Siquirres–Angostura contracts in 1879, and the decisive Soto–Keith contract of 1883 finally committed to the Cartago–Limón route that exists today — bypassing an earlier, more difficult proposal to run the line via Carrillo and La Palma over punishing terrain. The first through train from Limón to San José ran in December 1890, nineteen years after the project began.

The line was, by contemporary engineering accounts, built quickly and not always well. A government inspection in 1881 catalogued failures ranging from bridge abutments placed directly in riverbeds to rail fastened with half the specified number of rivets — criticism that foreshadowed decades of landslide and washout damage on the Turrialba–La Junta stretch in particular, the section nearest all three of these stations. It is a measure of the railway's importance to the country that, despite this troubled construction record, it remained the principal artery for Costa Rican coffee for over a century.
