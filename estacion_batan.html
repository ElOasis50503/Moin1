The Abacá Plantation

BAT-001 1905

Plantío de Abacá, Batán a Manila

Railway right-of-way through Manila-hemp cultivation

The railway's right-of-way disappears into a near-total green tunnel, the broad arching leaves of mature abacá plants closing overhead almost completely above the narrow gauge track. A single human figure is visible far down the line, dwarfed by the scale of the cultivation on either side — the only clear indication of distance and scale in an otherwise enveloping wall of foliage. The track itself runs perfectly straight, the engineered geometry of the railway cutting a clean line through what would otherwise read as undifferentiated jungle.

Abacá, or Manila hemp (Musa textilis), is a fiber-producing relative of the banana, native to the Philippines and historically that country's near-monopoly export crop before commercial cultivation spread to a small number of other countries — Costa Rica and Ecuador chief among them. Unlike the banana proper, abacá is grown not for fruit but for the strong, salt-resistant fiber extracted from its leaf-stems, historically prized for maritime rope and cordage and later for specialty papers. This plate's 1905 date places it early in whatever window Costa Rica's abacá cultivation occupied on this stretch of the Caribbean lowlands — a less prominent crop in the historical record than coffee, cacao, or the banana that would soon dominate the same corridor, but evidently substantial enough here to swallow the railway whole.

Batán: A Different Banana Relative

Batán sits squarely within the Caribbean lowland banana zone that defines most of this stretch of the line — but the photograph gathered here records a different, less celebrated relative of the fruit crop that would soon come to dominate the corridor. Abacá, or Manila hemp, is botanically a banana (Musa textilis), but it produces no edible fruit worth harvesting; its value lies entirely in the fiber extracted from its leaf-stems, a strong, salt-resistant material historically prized above ordinary hemp for maritime rope and cordage, and later repurposed into specialty papers — tea bags, filter paper, and banknote stock among them.

The crop's global history was, for most of the nineteenth century, very nearly a Philippine monopoly; commercial cultivation spread to only a small number of other countries through the twentieth century, with Costa Rica becoming one of the few outside the Philippines to grow it at meaningful scale, alongside Ecuador. Costa Rica's abacá industry has, in fact, continued in a modest way into the present day, with modern agricultural research particularly interested in its compatibility with agroforestry systems — a crop that can be integrated into a working forest rather than requiring it be cleared. This 1905 plate, dated early enough to predate banana's full dominance of the Caribbean lowlands, suggests abacá may have had a more substantial presence in this corridor's early plantation economy than its modest place in the historical record might otherwise indicate.

The hub's own line table places Batán at 41.2 km / 26 miles from Limón, between the Estrada junction and Pacuarito further down the line — geographically unremarkable, but agriculturally a useful reminder that the Caribbean lowland economy this railway served was never quite as single-minded as "the banana republic" framing sometimes suggests.
