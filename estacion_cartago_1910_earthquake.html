On 4 May 1910 an earthquake leveled much of Cartago — Costa Rica's old colonial capital. A National Geographic Magazine correspondent's photographs, published a month later, remain among the most widely reproduced visual records of the disaster.

About this spoke: Cartago: 1910 Earthquake sits alongside Cartago and Cartago: Side Trip to the Orosí Valley as the third entry under the Cartago slot in this index — a distinct historical episode rather than a station description, gathered here because it so completely reshaped the town that the railway's own station and surrounding infrastructure passed through.

The Streets

CART1910-001 4 May 1910 · published June 1910

Una Calle Principal Después del Terremoto

Cartago, Cartago Province — principal street, post-earthquake
One of the principal streets in Cartago after the earthquake of May 4, 1910.

A long street of single-storey buildings runs into the distance, their roofs collapsed and façades fractured along almost the entire visible block. A streetlamp hangs intact from its bracket above the rubble — an odd survival amid the wreckage — while a utility pole leans at the street's edge, still standing. Three figures, two women in dark mourning-style dress and a man in a light jacket, stand in the cobbled street near the worst of the damage, giving the photograph a human scale that the wider ruin alone would not convey.

This plate appeared on page 505 of the article, immediately following the magazine's introductory text, making it effectively the reader's first visual encounter with the earthquake's aftermath in the published piece.

The School

CART1910-002 4 May 1910 · published June 1910

Una Escuela para Señoritas

Cartago, Cartago Province — girls' school, collapsed upper storey
A school for young ladies in Cartago. The pupils and teachers were crushed in the ruins.

A substantial two-storey stone or masonry school building has lost its upper floor and roofline almost entirely along the photographed section, the corniced parapet sheared away and roof timbers splayed open to the sky. The ground floor's arched windows and heavy rusticated stonework remain largely intact, throwing the violence of the upper-storey collapse into sharper relief. Rubble is piled along the building's base.

The published caption states directly that pupils and teachers were killed when the building came down — one of the few captions in the sequence to state a human cost explicitly rather than leaving it to the image alone. The photograph is credited "Photo from J. Fid Tristán," the same Tristán whose photographic holdings form the basis of much of this project's other Costa Rican archival material, making this a useful early data point for Tristán's documented activity.

The Cemetery

CART1910-003 4 May 1910 · published June 1910

El Cementerio de Cartago

Cartago, Cartago Province — burial vaults, earthquake damage
The cemetery at Cartago, showing the vaults burst open by the earthquake.

A tiered wall of masonry burial niches, several rows high, has been split open along much of its length, leaving individual vaults exposed to view. The published caption is direct about what the photograph shows, and the image itself bears that out at close inspection — visible among the rubble and broken niches are coffin fragments and skeletal remains, including at least two visible skulls, alongside several figures standing among the ruins, apparently surveying the damage.

This is presented as a direct historical record from a published, credited source rather than as illustrative or decorative material — a sober document of one of the earthquake's most visceral consequences, the disturbance of the dead alongside the destruction inflicted on the living town.

Cartago, 1910: A Capital Undone

Cartago had been Costa Rica's colonial capital and remained, in 1910, one of its most architecturally significant towns — a place where Spanish colonial urban planning, nineteenth-century civic ambition, and a still-recent rail connection to the capital coexisted in a compact, densely built core. The earthquake of 4 May 1910 ended that built record almost completely. H. Pittier's National Geographic account, published a month later under the article title "Costa Rica — Vulcan's Smithy," gave English-language readers an early and widely circulated visual record of the destruction, and the three plates gathered here — a street, a school, a cemetery — sketch the disaster's reach across the full span of civic life: the everyday public way, an institution of learning, and the resting place of the dead.

The school photograph's caption is unusual in this set for stating a death toll explicitly rather than leaving the human cost to inference — pupils and teachers killed when the building's upper storey came down — and its credit to J. Fid Tristán connects this earthquake record directly to the Tristán fondo that anchors much of this project's other archival holdings from the Archivo Nacional de Costa Rica, though this particular image reaches the collection via the magazine rather than the archive itself.

Geographically, this episode sits squarely within the same Cartago that anchors the Atlantic Railway's station at mile 90 (see the main Cartago spoke) and the side trip to Orosí documented elsewhere in this index — the same town, the same province, three different lenses on it: infrastructure, agriculture and colonial church-building, and now catastrophe. The rail line itself, only twenty years into through-service from Limón to San José when the earthquake struck, would have carried the relief, the building materials, and very likely many of the dead and displaced in the disaster's immediate aftermath, though no specific record of that role has yet been identified for this catalogue.
