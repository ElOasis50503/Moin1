Mile 4 on the line out of Limón — the first stop on the Atlantic Railway, and the place where the railway's narrow strip of cleared right-of-way gave way, over the following century, to one of the country's busiest industrial sites.

About this spoke: Moín sits at the very start of the line, just north of Limón itself, and this page documents two distinct chapters of its history. The first is the railway corridor proper — track, palm groves, and right-of-way in the years when this was simply a stop on the line to Limón. The second is the site's later industrial life as a thermal power plant and bulk-cargo port, documented at length in the companion catalogue Moín: Port and Power, 1975–1991, linked from this page rather than duplicated here.

Between Moin and Estrada

A track-level view looking straight down the line, the rails receding to a vanishing point between dense walls of coconut palm and cleared grass embankment on either side — the characteristic low-country corridor of the railway's first stretch out of Limón. A single figure stands at the right edge of the frame, head turned from the camera, giving the only sense of scale and human presence in an otherwise empty cut through the plantation landscape.

The original caption situates the view between Moín and Estrada — the first two stops north and inland of the Limón terminus — placing it within the first five to ten kilometers of track on the line's full run to San José. The palms lining the route are consistent with the coconut and banana plantations that dominated this stretch of lowland Caribbean coast through much of the railway's working life.

Moín: First Stop, Last Word

Moín occupies a curious position in the Atlantic Railway's story: it is both the first stop out of Limón heading inland and, in a sense, the place where the line's original purpose has most thoroughly given way to something else. In the railway's working life, Moín was simply mile 4 — a stretch of cleared right-of-way through coconut and banana plantation country, a brief pause before the line settled into the long low-country run toward Estrada, Matina, and the rest of the Caribbean lowlands. The photograph gathered here under "The Corridor" belongs to that era: track, palms, grass embankment, and a single figure glimpsed at the frame's edge, unremarkable in the way that most of a 165-kilometer railway is unremarkable — simply the corridor doing its job.

What makes Moín distinct from its neighboring stops is what came after the trains stopped defining it. Where stations like Juan Viñas and Turrialba grew up around mills and plantations that depended on rail to move their cargo, Moín's twentieth-century significance shifted to a different kind of infrastructure entirely: the Instituto Costarricense de Electricidad's Moín I thermal plant, built from 1975 as a stopgap against a national energy crisis, and a bulk-cargo port handling petroleum, grain, and the export banana trade that once would have moved by rail. That later chapter — substantial enough to merit its own four-plate catalogue — is acknowledged here rather than duplicated, so that this spoke can do what spoke pages are meant to do: hold the station's place in the line's geography while pointing onward to where the fuller story lives.

The hub's own mileage table places Moín at 5.7 km / 4 miles from Limón — a short enough distance that, on a slow train, the corridor photographed here might have passed in a matter of minutes. That brevity is itself part of the point: Moín's importance was never really about how long the train lingered there, but about what grew up beside the tracks once the engineers moved on.
